# Microgrid LLM Coordination — Project Overview

**Description.** A research project asking: can a population of LLM agents, one per household, negotiate peer-to-peer to allocate scarce energy during a grid outage in a way that is (a) fair across heterogeneous households, (b) robust to incomplete or incorrect information, and (c) explainable to residents? The contribution is on the CS/ML axis (natural-language coordination, robustness, explainability), not power systems — classical optimization handles fairness under strong assumptions but struggles with robustness and doesn't attempt explainability. That gap is what the paper fills.

**Architecture.** A discrete-time energy simulator at `academic/microgrid/`. Thirty households on a 5×6 spatial grid; each has solar PV, a battery, residential load, and a grid connection. Houses share energy through a shared neighborhood transformer (50 kW cap, 5% transit loss). Five Python modules under `sim/`: `types`, `household` (battery physics — rate clamps, RT efficiency, DoD floor), `data` (load/solar adapters), `network` (comm graph + transfer settlement), plus `engine`/`logging`/`strategies` to come. The coordination strategy is an injected callback — Phase 2 swaps in LLM agents without touching the simulator core.

**Plan.** Four phases, ~6-8 months. Phase 1 (current, 25 TDD tasks, 32% complete): physics-only simulator. Phase 2: LLM agents with memory, reflection, and natural-language messaging. Phase 3: benchmark suite + fairness, served-load, and explanation-quality metrics. Phase 4: web visualization + workshop paper (ICLR Climate / NeurIPS CompSus / AAMAS).
